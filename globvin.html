Understanding the condition

What actually happens
when you have anemia?

Anemia is not simply "feeling tired." It is a fundamental deficiency in your blood's ability to carry oxygen — because your body either lacks enough red blood cells, or those cells don't contain enough hemoglobin to function properly.

Every organ in your body depends on a steady supply of oxygen delivered by red blood cells. When that supply is compromised — due to iron deficiency, poor nutrition, blood loss, or bone marrow issues — every system suffers. Fatigue, paleness, shortness of breath, weakness and poor concentration are all symptoms of oxygen-starved tissues.

1

Iron deficiency — the most common cause

Without enough iron, your body cannot produce hemoglobin — the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen. Low hemoglobin = low oxygen = constant fatigue.

2

Red blood cells become too few or too small

Iron-deficient red blood cells are smaller and paler than normal. They carry less oxygen and die faster — the body can't keep up with production and a deficit builds.

3

Every organ begins to suffer

Heart works harder. Brain gets less oxygen. Muscles fatigue faster. Immune system weakens. In children — cognitive development is affected. In women — periods worsen the deficit further.

4
