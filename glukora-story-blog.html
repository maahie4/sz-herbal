The night it happened

She had been in labor for hours. The hospital team was ready. The surgical team was ready. Everything was prepared for an emergency C-section to deliver the baby safely.

But there was one problem. Her blood sugar was 380 mg/dL.

For a surgical procedure — especially a C-section — blood sugar must be stable and within a safe range before the team can operate. At 380, the risks are severe: poor wound healing, infection, anaesthesia complications, and danger to both mother and child.

Why blood sugar matters in surgery

High blood glucose impairs the body's ability to clot blood, fight infection, and restore tissue. In C-section surgery, uncontrolled blood sugar significantly raises the risk of surgical site infection, delayed recovery, and complications with anaesthesia. Doctors follow strict protocols — they will not operate until glucose is within a safe range. This is not a choice. It is patient safety.

The doctors worked. They administered insulin. They adjusted her medications. They monitored her closely. But the sugar was not coming down quickly enough — and the baby could not wait indefinitely.

Hours passed. The family stood outside the ward. Her husband — a man who had already done everything he could think of — was running out of options. And running out of time.

The phone call
